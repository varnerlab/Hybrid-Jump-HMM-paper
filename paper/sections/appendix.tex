\section{Empirical cross-term diagnostic}
\label{sec:supp-cov}

The variance-correction in Section~\ref{sec:method-variance} rests on
the assumption $\Cov[\teps_i, g_m] \approx 0$, which is what allows
the derivation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:scale} to drop the cross-term in the
expansion of $\Var[g_i]$. Because the per-asset JumpHMM marginals are
fit to each asset's full growth-rate history, including its
market-correlated component, the assumption is not automatic: a
generator that picked up a residual market loading would invalidate
the closed-form scaling. We checked the assumption empirically by
sampling $100$ innovation paths $\teps_i^{(r)}$ per ticker from each
generator and computing the normalized cross-covariance against the
real SPY path:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{cov}^{\mathrm{norm}}_i
  = \frac{1}{R} \sum_{r=1}^{R}
    \frac{\Cov[\teps_i^{(r)},\, g_m]}{\sigma_{\teps,i}\, \sigma_m}.
\end{equation}
Per-ticker values are plotted as histograms in
Fig.~\ref{fig:cov_diag}. The full-return JumpHMM generator
(\texttt{jumphmm\_full}, used by hybrid and naive) sits within
$\pm 0.05$ of zero with a slight rightward bias of order $10^{-2}$;
the residual-fit JumpHMM generator (\texttt{jumphmm\_residual}, used
by JumpHMM-on-residuals) and the Gaussian baseline are both centered
at zero with no detectable bias. The independence assumption is
therefore satisfied to within sampling tolerance for every composer
that uses it, and the derivation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:scale} holds in
practice.
